Borderlands

Evidence & methodological pluralism in Kenya‚ÄìUganda border analysis

Overview

Led a computational policy research project on trade, conflict, and kinship dynamics along the Kenya‚ÄìUganda border. The project combined spatial modeling with a critical reflection on how computational evidence becomes actionable in fragile contexts, emphasizing the need for co-construction with decision-makers.

What I Built

Market Potential Index

Trade connectivity analysis via roads, markets, and population reach to identify economic opportunity zones.

Conflict Exposure Index

Risk mapping using geocoded ACLED data to quantify security dynamics along the border.

Relational Layer

Cross-border kinship, language, and trust networks shaping informal trade routes and social cohesion.

Critical Essay

Authored a reflexive paper on translating technical evidence into institutional decision-making.

Key Findings

  • High-risk/high-reward corridors: Identified Busia & Malaba as zones with significant trade potential but elevated conflict exposure
  • Overlooked stable zones: Kaabong and Amudat emerged as underinvested areas with lower risk profiles
  • Methodological insight: Standalone computational models require co-defined policy questions to be actionable
  • Broader contribution: Advanced dialogue on reflexive, collaborative computational research in development

Policy Impact

The project demonstrated how spatial analytics can inform alternative policy investment strategies in fragile border regions. The accompanying essay contributed to methodological debates about the role of evidence in governance and the importance of legibility and co-construction.
